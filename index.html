What You See is What You GIF:
Preview-based Authoring for Data-GIFs

This work presents DataGifify, a lightweight authoring tool for data-GIFs. Data-GIFs are a popular and engaging medium on social media, which convey a brief message in short and repeated animations with visualization. However, they are currently challenging to create due to the lack of specialized tools. Existing authoring tools support creating chart animations by providing predefined templates or customizing keyframe animations. However, they appear like ``heavy artillery'' for creating short and concise GIFs, and do not capture the unique designs of data-GIFs. Inspired by short video effects and editing tools, we contribute DataGifify, which leverages the design space of data-GIFs and introduces narrative effects applied to visualization animation. Moreover, DataGifify follows a preview-based authoring paradigm that supports faceted browsing of dynamic previews with various narrative effects. The step-wise interface in DataGifify guides users through uploading data, choosing visualization designs, applying narrative effects, customizing timing, and export. To validate the tool, we present a gallery of data-GIFs using real-world datasets. We conduct a user study investigating the usability and usefulness of DataGifify through reproduction and free-form creation. Our results show that users can quickly learn to use our tool and easily create a variety of data-GIFs.

Preliminary Study

The goal of the preliminary study is to investigate the current practices for data-GIF design and authoring approaches, which in turn informs the design goals for DataGifify. The study goes through three main steps, i.e., re-implementing existing data-GIFs, creating data-GIFs, follow-up interviews.

1) Re-implementing existing data-GIFs

In this step, we began by analyzing collected data-GIFs and re-implementing examples by two of the authors via programming. The re-implementation of existing data-GIFs helped us identify recurring design components, differentiate nuanced details of animation, and gauge a proper starting point from the huge design space of data-GIFs, i.e., temporal data-GIFs.

2) Creating data-GIFs

In the second step, We recruited participants in a Data Visualization course taken by third-year undergraduate students majoring in Digital Media to design and generate temporal data-GIFs. We assigned a bonus in the course and students submitted the bonus on a voluntary basis. As a result, 40 students (37.0%) finished the bonus. Below shows four selected GIFs created by students. Other qualified GIFs can be downloaded through the link.

3) Follow-up interview

We conducted a semi-structured interview with eight students (S1-S8) who submitted qualified GIFs with aesthetic and novel designs. The findings can be found on the paper.

User Study

To understand if our approach to Preview-based authoring and how our implementation in DataGifify supports authors in creating GIFs, we ran a qualitative user study.
